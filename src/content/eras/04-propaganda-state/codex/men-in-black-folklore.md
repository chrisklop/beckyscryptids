In 1953, Albert K. Bender — a Connecticut clerk who had been running an amateur UFO-investigation outfit called the *International Flying Saucer Bureau* — shut his operation down without warning. He told colleagues that three pale men in dark suits had appeared in his bedroom and instructed him to stop.

That account, written up in Gray Barker's 1956 book *They Knew Too Much About Flying Saucers*, established the template. Pale-skinned. Identical suits. Slow, courtly speech. They knew where you lived. They knew what you had been thinking. And they were always, somehow, *just a little wrong* — the smile not quite right, the wristwatches outdated, the car a model that hadn't been made for thirty years.

By the 1970s, every UFO witness with a tabloid budget had a Men in Black encounter. By 1997 they had two Hollywood movies. The folklore became the official version, which made the original encounters either more or less credible depending on whom you asked.
