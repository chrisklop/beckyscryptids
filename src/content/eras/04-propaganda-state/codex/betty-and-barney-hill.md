On the night of 19–20 September 1961, Betty and Barney Hill — a postal worker and a social worker, an interracial couple driving home through the White Mountains of New Hampshire — saw a strange light pacing their car. They arrived home two hours later than they should have, with no memory of the missing time.

Under hypnosis with Dr. Benjamin Simon two years later, both independently described being taken aboard a craft and examined by small grey humanoids. Betty drew a star map from memory. Years later an Ohio schoolteacher claimed Betty's star map matched the orientation of Zeta Reticuli — a claim that depends entirely on which stars you choose to include.

It was the first widely reported abduction narrative. Every element of the modern abduction script — the missing time, the medical exam, the small greys, the hypnotic recovery — was set down in the Hill case and copied forward.

The Hills themselves remained mild, sober, and unwavering. They were not selling a book. They were not on a podcast. They simply said it happened.
