In November 1951, the mountaineer Eric Shipton was descending from the Menlung Glacier in the eastern Himalaya when he photographed a 13-inch footprint in the snow with his ice axe alongside for scale. The print had a thick, divided big toe and four smaller toes. The photo ran around the world.

Shipton himself never claimed it was a yeti. He thought it was probably a langur or a bear, distorted by sun and meltwater. But the photo was *spectacular*. It was reprinted in every newspaper from Calcutta to Cleveland, often cropped to obscure the more ordinary prints around it.

Sherpa culture had always carried stories of the metoh-kangmi — the man-bear of the snows. Shipton's photograph gave the Western imagination something to hang those stories on, and the legend grew.
