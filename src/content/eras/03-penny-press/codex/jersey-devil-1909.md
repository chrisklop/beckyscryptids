Between January 16 and 23, 1909, the people of New Jersey, eastern Pennsylvania, and parts of Delaware reported a sustained, panicked week of encounters with a flying, hoofed, kangaroo-bodied creature with a horse's head and bat wings. Schools closed. Mills shut down. Posses formed.

Tracks were found in snow on rooftops. Dogs were attacked. One trolley conductor swore the thing chased his streetcar in Burlington.

The legend itself was over a century old by then — the Devil was supposedly the cursed 13th child of a colonial-era Mrs. Leeds, born around 1735. But the 1909 week, fanned across the front pages of every Philadelphia daily, fixed the modern image: the photo-grade illustrations the *Philadelphia Inquirer* commissioned shaped what every later "witness" reported seeing.

A press-driven mass sighting. A century later, the New Jersey Devils hockey team is named for it.
