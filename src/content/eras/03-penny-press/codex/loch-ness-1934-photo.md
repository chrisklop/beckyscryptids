On 21 April 1934, the *Daily Mail* published a grainy photograph of a long, curved neck rising from the surface of Loch Ness. The photographer was identified only as a respectable London surgeon — Robert Kenneth Wilson — which gave the picture an air of unimpeachable credibility.

The image defined the popular idea of Nessie for sixty years. Every cryptozoologist studied it. Every documentary opened with it.

In 1994, on his deathbed, Wilson's stepson Christian Spurling confessed: the "monster" was a toy submarine fitted with a sculpted plasticene head, photographed in shallow water. The whole thing was revenge on the *Daily Mail* by a man named Marmaduke Wetherell, whose earlier "footprints" had been exposed as hippo-toe umbrella stands.

Nessie has not been photographed half as convincingly since. Becky maintains there is still something down there.
