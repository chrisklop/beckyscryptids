Homer's sirens lived on a flowering meadow ringed by the bones of dead sailors. Pliny the Elder swore they existed. Medieval bestiaries kept Pliny on the books for fifteen hundred years.

In the Age of Sail, every becalmed crew that drifted onto rocks had a story ready: the song, the women on the headland, the way the helmsman just couldn't help himself. The tale was easier to publish than to admit the captain had been drunk and the chart was bad.

Sirens are how the sea launders a bad decision. They have done it reliably since Odysseus, and ship's insurers eventually stopped asking.
