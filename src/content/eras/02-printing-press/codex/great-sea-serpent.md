On July 6, 1734, the Lutheran missionary Hans Egede was sailing along the western coast of Greenland when, according to his report, *"a most terrible creature… raised itself so high above the water that its head reached above our maintop."* He sketched it. The sketch shows a serpent with a long, narrow snout, scaly body, and a tail that vanishes below the waves.

Egede was a sober man in a fur cassock. He had no reason to lie. The drawing — copied and recopied — fixed the image of the "great sea serpent" in the European imagination for the next two centuries. Every later sighting from Gloucester to the Hebrides was compared to it.

The most likely real animal? Probably a giant squid breaching, or a beaked whale rolling. But the story always wins on a slow news week.
