Slavic grandmothers warned of Baba Yaga: a hag who lived deep in the birch forest in a hut perched on enormous chicken legs. The hut turned to face whoever spoke the right words. Her fence was bone. Her broom was a pestle.

She was sometimes a tester of heroes, sometimes a devourer of children. The story did the work of keeping kids out of the woods at dusk — and gave the village a name to whisper when a neighbor's bread didn't rise.
